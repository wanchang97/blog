    • Foreword: A Spontaneous and Surprising Journey\nThis winter, my mother and I spent two “Zen-like” weeks in Serbia. From wandering through Belgrade to an impromptu ski week in Kopaonik, this trip, initially a spontaneous decision due to visa issues, brought us unexpected joy.\nThe cost of living here is extremely low, and contrary to rumors, there’s no sense of unrest; instead, it’s imbued with a distinctive, righteous sense of security peculiar to socialist countries.\n\n# History and Present: Playing All Sides After the Smoke Clears\nOver the past century, this region has been plagued by continuous wars, conflicts among three major religions, and successive struggles for dominance among empires. Even immediately after landing, you can see “Kosovo is Serbia” slogans, as if a single spark could ignite another conflict. Border disputes persist after the 2008 Kosovo War of Independence.\n\nA stunning statistic: the capital Belgrade has historically endured 115 wars and been razed to the ground 44 times, an unparalleled record in global urban history. Walking through Belgrade’s Old Town, it’s hard to ignore the black marks on building facades and the severed tree branches. Not all of them speak of shelling. Some are indeed the scorched scars left by the 1999 airstrikes, still standing in the city center like solidified pain; however, many more are the accumulation of soot, exhaust fumes, and years of oxidation over a century. These varying shades of gray and black cling to the exquisite Austro-Hungarian style carvings, making the entire city resemble a faded charcoal drawing. This is completely unlike the usual quaint and elegant European towns one might see. I cannot imagine what Ukraine, where the war is still ongoing, must look like now.\n\nYet, precisely because it stands at the crossroads of East-West geopolitical games, Serbia can simultaneously integrate three religious beliefs and accommodate diverse political views. Present-day Serbia adeptly navigates between multiple powers. Despite varied internal opinions, its friendly relations with China and Russia, coupled with its openness to the EU and the US, inject strong impetus into its economic growth. Not only has Serbia offered visa-free entry to ordinary Chinese passport holders since 2017, and actively sought to join the EU, but it’s also remarkably friendly towards the US. For instance, in late 2025, the Serbian Parliament just passed a proposal, with 130 votes to 40, to construct a Trump hotel on the former site of the Yugoslav Ministry of Defense building, which was bombed by NATO. This project also received personal backing from Serbian President Vučić, who designated the reconstruction as an emergency project and lifted the cultural heritage protection status of the site. This incident sparked strong local discontent and protests. Whether this indicates a lack of principles, a disregard for historical preservation, or flexible pragmatism focused on economic development, opinions vary. One can sense that museums, historical culture, and similar aspects here lack the same steadfast preservation and heritage seen in Europe; often, government-led museum renovations only begin after multiple rounds of protests. But then, how can I criticize a nation repeatedly ravaged by war, constantly migrating and seeking refuge, for not understanding how to protect and uphold its own culture? At the same time, there are indeed many voices today that believe Europe is generally too rigid and inflexible, leading to slower development.\n\nHere, we’ll set aside current affairs for now and focus on practical travel tips to help you “pack up and go.” The following Chenhe’s Blog - Serbia Ski Guide was incredibly helpful when I planned my itinerary. Inspired by it, I want to build upon it by adding my own 2026 Serbia travel and skiing experiences, hoping to provide some reference for friends considering a trip to Serbia.\n\n# Serbia Travel Essentials: Pre-Trip Preparations\n\n## 🛂 Entry and Visa\nVisa-free for Chinese passports, the process is extremely smooth. Essentially no queues, no difficulties, just direct stamping upon entry. Currently, Serbia is a non-Schengen country located in the Balkans region of Europe, so flying directly from China does not require a Schengen visa.\n\n## 📶 Internet and SIM Cards\n### International Roaming with Domestic Carriers — Not Recommended\n1. Signal Issues: Roaming signals are unstable and frequently degrade.\n2. “The Great Firewall” Still Exists: Using domestic roaming prevents direct access to Google Maps, ChatGPT, or Gemini, which is fatal for independent travel (checking maps/instant translation).\nTherefore, it’s better to temporarily connect to airport WiFi and then purchase a local SIM card at the airport.\n\n### International Travel eSIM Plan — Convenient and Flexible, Slightly Higher Price\nSuitable for multi-country travel arrangements. Generally, current domestic mobile phones use physical dual-SIM cards and do not support eSIMs, while US versions use entirely virtual cards and cannot use physical cards. European versions of phones typically have one physical and one virtual card. Please check your phone type to confirm eSIM support.\n\n### Purchase a Local Physical SIM Card or eSIM\n* Where to Buy: After landing in Serbia, you’ll find Yettel, A1, and other operator counters at the airport baggage claim. Prices are transparent, no price gouging, and activation is done on-site with your passport.\n* Package Reference (February 2026):\n * Serbia Only: \n For example, Yettel offers tourist-friendly packages like 100GB data for 700 RSD for 15 days; 50GB data for 500 RSD for 7 days, etc.\n\n * Balkan Roaming: If you plan to visit Montenegro or Bosnia and Herzegovina, you can choose 50GB (within Serbia) + 8GB (roaming) for 1400 RSD (30 days). Note: These types of packages sometimes require visiting a city branch for activation.\n* Call Needs: The above packages are mostly data-only. If you need to contact your host, consider downloading the operator’s app to top up for per-minute billing; or use WhatsApp, which consumes data.\n\n## 🚌 Transportation\n\n* Airport Bus (A1 Shuttle): Requires an additional payment (approx. 400 RSD/person) and goes directly to the city center. The vehicles are older but very fast.\n* City Buses: Starting January 1, 2025, all public transportation in Serbia is free (including for tourists). Buses are frequent; although often crowded, they are very convenient.\n* Long-Distance Buses: Google Maps often fails to display transportation options between different towns, frequently showing only driving or walking routes. Intercity buses in Serbia are operated by dozens of private companies (e.g., Lasta, Jugoprevoz, Fudeks). Most of these companies do not make real-time or static timetables available in the format required by Google.\n Alternatives:\n 1. BAS Official Booking Website\n 2. However, for buses operated by companies like Lasta, booked online, you might need to purchase an additional platform ticket at the BAS station, which costs approximately 500 RSD per person. If you buy tickets directly at the bus station, you won’t need to pay the extra platform fee, but the total price might be similar.\n 3. Kopaonik Express Bus:\n In addition to regular buses, many private minibuses/shared transfers (e.g., Terra Travel or Gea Tours) operate routes to and from Kopaonik. They offer “door-to-door” service (picking up from your Belgrade accommodation and dropping you off at the ski resort). These services are not searchable on any map software and must be booked through their official websites or via phone/WhatsApp.\n* Taxis: We recommend using Yandex Go or Pink Taxi. Prices are transparent, and you can effectively avoid being overcharged by flagging down a taxi on the street.\n\n## 💰 Currency Exchange and Payment\nThe Serbian currency is the Dinar (RSD), with an exchange rate relatively stable against the Euro. Euros are also widely accepted. However, many prefer cash for payments, especially in guesthouses, so it’s essential to carry enough cash.\n\n* Strategy: Carry Euro cash + a multi-currency credit card.\n* Currency Exchange: There are automatic currency exchange machines at the airport baggage claim, with transparent exchange rates.\n* Scenarios Requiring Cash: Airport shuttle, long-distance buses, taxis, some Booking guesthouse fees, ski equipment rental, instructor fees, and small restaurants.\n* Budget Reference: For two people traveling for two weeks, exchanging around 500 Euros into RSD cash is usually sufficient.\n\n## 🧳 Luggage Packing: Layered Clothing and German Two-Round-Pin Plug\n* Climate: Winters are mostly above freezing, but incredibly windy! Where the Sava and Danube rivers meet, the swirling winds are wild, more exhilarating than at the seaside.\n* Attire: Recommend “onion-style” layering; bring everything from short-sleeved shirts to shell jackets.\n* For Skiers (Must Read): Snowboard beginners are advised to bring their own professional hip and knee protectors (avoid cheap “Taobao turtle shells,” which easily shift and offer insufficient protection). In Belgrade, there are a few Decathlon stores, but choices for professional ski shops are limited; good equipment mostly relies on online shopping.\n\n# Exploring and Dining in Belgrade: Markets, Sports Matches, Yugoslav Drama Theatre\n\nFor general historical and cultural background, you can ask AI. Here, I’ll only share places that I found enjoyable. We wanted to experience more of local life, so we didn’t opt for luxurious places like hotels or spas. Instead, we chose guesthouses, local swimming pools, bakeries popular with locals, and markets. We didn’t take taxis; instead, we relied more on public buses and walking.\n\n## 🏠 Accommodation Layout and Recommendations\nI booked two different guesthouses in Belgrade via Booking; they offered excellent value for money, so you can choose based on your preferences.\n\nBelgrade city center is at the confluence of the Sava and Danube rivers (Ušće, meaning “confluence”). The Sava River divides Belgrade into east and west sides.\n\nBelgrade Area Comparison\n| Area | Characteristics | Suitable For |\n| :— | :— | :— |\n| Old Town (East Side) | Crowded but lively, similar to Barcelona. Water polo is the national sport of Serbia.\nOfficial ticketing website: Belgrade 2026\nIf sold out, you can check the secondary market website viagogo.\n> 💡 Tips: On viagogo, ticket prices often drop significantly around seven hours before the match. You might not need to rush to buy tickets on secondary sites; you can often find last-minute deals.\n\n\n#### Tašmajdan Pool\nWe visited a swimming pool here, which offers citizen prices: 600 RSD/person for three hours. It closes for lunch, with separate opening hours for morning and afternoon. If you want a gym, swimming, and spa experience, it’s 2200 RSD/person.\n\nIt was interesting that although the pool is Olympic-sized (50 meters long), people don’t swim the full length; instead, they swim across the short width. Everyone occupies a short end and swims back and forth.\n\n## Culture and Performances\n### JDP (Yugoslav Drama Theatre)\n\nYou can check the program schedule at https://www.jdp.rs/. Often, tickets shown online are sold out, so it’s worth inquiring at the offline box office. The performances are innovative, authentic, and unconventional. The language is usually Serbian, with some productions including English subtitles mid-show. While you might not understand everything, the body language, movements, music, and stage design are all very immersive.\n\n### Museums\nThe Museum of Contemporary Art is the sawtooth-shaped building located in Ušće Park; it’s free on the first Wednesday of every month and annually on May 18th.\nThe National Museum of Serbia is free every Sunday.\n\n## Food and Specialty Recommendations\n\n#### Belgrade’s Markets (Pijaca), Fish Market, and Corner Bakeries\n\n##### Belgrade’s Green Markets\nKalenić Green Market (Kalenić Pijaca)\nTry local homemade Kajmak (clotted cream) and Ajvar (pepper relish).\n\n##### Belgrade’s Fish Market\nZemun Farmers Market (Zemunska Pijaca)\n\n\n##### Bakeries (Pekara)\nThe freshest bread is often found at a random corner bakery. Just find any Pekara on a street corner and buy a sesame-crusted Đevrek (bagel), which is very similar to a Turkish Simit. Paired with a bottle of pink cow yogurt, it’s the soul of a local breakfast.\n\n#### Belgrade Local Cuisine\n\nA Michelin-recommended restaurant, Iva New Balkan Cuisine, specializes in innovative preparations of Serbian local dishes like meats and dairy. Prices are moderate, and reservations are required at least one day in advance. Lunch orders start at noon.\n\nA Michelin-starred restaurant, Langouste, requires advance booking. A no-show after booking will incur a 3000 RSD charge.\n\n#### River Fish Restaurants in Zemun District\nOne is the century-old restaurant Šaran. Prices are on the higher side, but the food is excellent, with fish soup and whole fish. We recommend the fresh river fish caught from the nearby Danube.\n\n\nAnother is a casual fish shop, Fish Butik. They only prepare food upon order, and it’s all for takeaway, but the prices are very good. Local residents often order meals here and pick them up after half an hour. Their menu is in Serbian, so we primarily ordered based on photos and translation.\nHere, you can choose from four types of river fish:\n* carp\n* catfish\n* trout\n* perch\nAfter choosing, you can select the cooking method: grilled or fried.\n还可以选择配菜或者配土豆。You can also choose side dishes or potatoes.\nThis shop only accepts cash. If you eat there directly, the owner will borrow two forks from next door.\n\n#### Belgrade Chinese Food\nSeveral Chinese restaurants we casually entered were unexpectedly delicious and authentic. (Though we don’t have very high standards for food, perhaps Chinese cuisine is simply more satisfying.)\nThere are many options like dumplings, roujiamo, liangpi, ramen, hotpot, and malatang.\n\n\n# Skiing in Kopaonik: A Beginner’s Guide\n\nMy skiing advice is more suited for beginners, as we didn’t explore the entire mountain all week, focusing mainly on the beginner conveyor belts. We went up the mountain twice and did one night ski session with a coach. Both my mother and I are quite timid, and it was our first time learning to ski—my mother on parallel skis, and me on a snowboard. We encountered quite a few challenges, but I’ve written them down for reference.\n\n## Insurance\nWhile buying insurance, we naturally hope not to make a claim, yet it allows us to embrace new experiences with peace of mind. I recommend arranging two types of insurance: Travel Insurance and Ski Insurance.\n\n### Travel Insurance\nTravel insurance varies greatly, and my mother’s situation and mine were also different. Specifically, before her trip to visit me in Germany, my mother had only purchased Schengen area insurance, which meant I couldn’t buy her travel insurance for European residents traveling abroad, nor for Chinese mainland residents traveling abroad (because most travel insurance policies must be purchased before departure, and by then my mother was already in Germany).\n\nFinally, with the help of Gemini, we found several travel insurance providers that cater to those whose trips have already begun, regardless of residency. We purchased Heymondo, checking the “already abroad” option. It requires a 72-hour waiting period after purchase before it becomes effective. Since we didn’t need to use it, I don’t know the specifics of their claims process. It simply offered a feasible solution for adding insurance for a trip already underway. In the future, it’s always best to purchase global travel insurance domestically; for a similar price, it can save a lot of hassle.\n\nFor those residing long-term in Europe, travel insurance prices usually differ depending on whether the destination includes the US and Canada. Also, a one-time travel insurance policy isn’t necessarily cheaper than an annual one. It can also be more cost-effective for couples or families to buy together. You can compare options on check24. This time, I purchased an annual worldwide policy with Europe Assistance.\n\n### Ski Insurance\n\nGenerally, purchasing a ski pass for the ski lift will include insurance for that day at the ski resort. Some ski resorts might charge separately for this. However, this local coverage is indeed more direct and reliable.\n\nFor frequent skiers residing in Europe, you can consider purchasing ski insurance from DSV (Deutsche Ski Verein). They offer packages that cover ski travel-related risks worldwide, including damage or loss of ski equipment, and third-party liability for injuring others. This might be the most cost-effective insurance type I’ve found.\n\nSki equipment rentals sometimes also incur additional insurance fees. If you have DSV insurance, you don’t need to purchase extra insurance from the ski shop. Otherwise, it’s advisable to get comprehensive coverage. Kopaonik’s ski center is relatively casual; the rental shop I used didn’t mention ski equipment insurance. Sometimes they take a photo of your ID, sometimes they just ask for your ski instructor’s name, and typically there’s no deposit.\n\n## Accommodation, Transportation, and Ski Equipment Rental:\n\nConsider accommodation, transportation, and ski equipment rental together with your skiing level.\nIf you’re looking for value for money and staying in a nearby town, requiring driving or taking a bus to and from the slopes, you can rent equipment at the Ski Center and store it there daily.\n\n### Accommodation\nIf you want a ski-in ski-out experience, especially as a beginner, the main beginner conveyor belts are located at the Kopaonik Ski Center. Therefore, when booking a hotel, it’s best to stay near Ski Center Kopaonik. Notable four-star hotels here include Gorski. You can also find nearby guesthouses through Booking, which might be more reasonably priced and include amenities like washing machines and kitchens. The ski center has affordable chain supermarkets like Maxi and Idea, making daily life convenient even without breakfast provided.\nInfoKop\nThis website compiles all useful information about skiing in Kopaonik, including weather, activities, ski pass prices, and last-minute accommodation rates. Although last-minute information is only in Serbian, the English section provides ample, up-to-date details on lift operations and slope conditions, which are essential for skiers.\n\n### Transportation\nTransportation at the ski resort includes taxis, ski buses, self-driving, and cable cars. Taxis are a bit expensive and might overcharge, which I didn’t dare to try. Ski buses run once an hour, and tickets are bought with cash on board. Cable cars operate continuously during daytime hours.\n\nIf you are an experienced skier, capable of comfortably using T-bar and platter lifts, there are many ski accommodations to choose from in nearby villages. For details on village guesthouses, see Chenhe’s Blog. Even if they are located in towns different from the ski center, they can still offer a near ski-in ski-out experience. Obviously, beginners should not put this pressure on themselves.\n\nSince nearby ski buses run hourly and incur additional charges, if you really need to commute daily by ski bus, you can rent equipment at the Ski Center and store it there for free. To conserve energy and enhance the skiing experience, prioritize ski-in ski-out accommodation.\n\nAccommodation in Brzeće village is half the price of the Ski Center. Although it’s a nearby village, a new Gondola lift has been built, with more frequent services than the bus, allowing for ascent and descent. However, note that the last Gondola usually runs at 16:00, and after this time, you’ll need to take a ski bus back.\n\n### Ski Equipment Rental\nHere, I recommend Mašinac Resort, an accommodation and restaurant I saw while skiing. It’s located right next to Green Slope #22 and is more like a youth hostel. Due to its low prices and good location, it’s often fully booked. You can also rent equipment, hire instructors, and store your gear here. The prices are arguably the lowest on the entire mountain: ski rental is 800 RSD/day, snowboard is 1000 RSD/day, helmet 200 RSD/day, goggles 200 RSD/day.\n\n\nMany ski shops downhill have higher prices, though renting for several days can offer discounts. Below is a quote chart I got from a downhill ski shop.\n\n\nOf course, the lower prices come with lower equipment quality. If you don’t mind and just want to try it out, you can contact them directly. Their English isn’t great, but they are very enthusiastic. Their coaching fees are also quite low, around 30 Euros/hour. However, these are not officially certified instructors, even though they constantly take groups of young Serbians staying at the hostel up the mountain to learn skiing. The ski instructors here also offered us discounted ski passes (which is essentially illegal and risky, as the name on the ski pass wouldn’t be yours, meaning you wouldn’t be covered by insurance if an accident occurred). People in Eastern Europe often have a “many people can get away with running a red light” kind of mentality; rules and regulations don’t seem to be as restrictive, but this also leads to a vibrant and free-spirited atmosphere beyond rigidness.\n\nIf you prefer a ski shop with better quality equipment, my instructor recommended a place with moderate prices that was indeed quite professional, though it had more options for snowboards.\n\n> 💡 Tips: Although Slope #22 is a green run, it still requires using a platter lift to go up and down. Beginners might find it difficult to confidently use the platter lift. Therefore, when my mother and I rented equipment here, we still had to carry it back to the halfway point to return it.\n\n## Ski Pass and Instructors\nFor various ski pass combinations, you can check the InfoKop website. Purchasing online offers at least a 5% discount compared to buying on-site.\nHigh-Resolution Ski Map\nSki Pass Price List 2025-2026\nSki pass combinations are diverse, including single-use beginner tickets, half-day, full-day, night skiing, and multi-day passes. For night skiing, only Slope #7 and its lift are open. However, this is a blue run with excellent snow quality. There’s daily artificial snowmaking and grooming, and music at night creates a great atmosphere.\n\nThe beginner slopes here are all located near the ski center and use conveyor belts. There’s a very gentle “bunny slope,” and slightly sloped beginner runs as shown by the green millet-like sections in the image. The area enclosed by the BMW billboard in front is the bunny slope for parallel skiers. The sloped green-marked area in the image is specifically for beginner snowboarders. If you hire a ski instructor, you’ll usually meet them on the beginner slopes. A standard ski instructor’s fee is around 45 Euros/hour. If you also rent equipment from the same shop, you might get further discounts.\n\n\nI suggest buying beginner tickets for the first few days, then upgrading to half-day, multi-day passes, etc., as your skill improves! Undecided beginners can consult with their ski instructor to decide whether to go up the mountain or stay on the beginner slopes for the day.\n\n## Relaxation Activities: Spa & Wellness\nWhen learning to ski gets intense, it’s essential to know how to relax.\nWe tried the four-star Gorski Hotel, which advertises even in Belgrade. For non-hotel guests, it’s 3900 RSD/person for a morning session. You pay directly on the B3 floor. The pool and hot springs were decent, the saunas were complete, and there was a unique relaxation room with water beds, which was quite novel.\n\nOn the B1 floor of the Gorski Hotel, there’s an Italian Fine Dining restaurant. Portions are good, and they get creative with presentations. There’s a dessert featuring raspberries and rose petals, served with dry ice for a smoking effect, perfect for photos.

I recommend arranging two types of insurance: Travel Insurance and Ski Insurance.\n\n### Travel Insurance\nTravel insurance varies greatly, and my mother’s situation and mine were also different. Specifically, before her trip to visit me in Germany, my mother had only purchased Schengen area insurance, which meant I couldn’t buy her travel insurance for European residents traveling abroad, nor for Chinese mainland residents traveling abroad (because most travel insurance policies must be purchased before departure, and by then my mother was already in Germany).\n\nFinally, with the help of Gemini, we found several travel insurance providers that cater to those whose trips have already begun, regardless of residency. We purchased Heymondo, checking the “already abroad” option. It requires a 72-hour waiting period after purchase before it becomes effective. Since we didn’t need to use it, I don’t know the specifics of their claims process. It simply offered a feasible solution for adding insurance for a trip already underway. In the future, it’s always best to purchase global travel insurance domestically; for a similar price, it can save a lot of hassle.\n\nFor those residing long-term in Europe, travel insurance prices usually differ depending on whether the destination includes the US and Canada. Also, a one-time travel insurance policy isn’t necessarily cheaper than an annual one. It can also be more cost-effective for couples or families to buy together. You can compare options on check24. This time, I purchased an annual worldwide policy with Europe Assistance.\n\n### Ski Insurance\n\nGenerally, purchasing a ski pass for the ski lift will include insurance for that day at the ski resort. Some ski resorts might charge separately for this. This might be the most cost-effective insurance type I’ve found.\n\nSki equipment rentals sometimes also incur additional insurance fees.
