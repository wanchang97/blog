Introduction: Signals from the Body

My allergies are back. Why? Is it really just the allergens and the weather? Or is it here to protect me from something, to remind me of something?

I. Tension, Fantasies, and the Feeling of Oppression
Whenever I feel tense, I often fantasize: "If only I were extremely rich," so I wouldn't have to push myself so hard anymore. For instance, buying a massive winner stock, or making some legendary investment decision.

At times, I want to make money so badly—to have my own career, a stable income, and social recognition. At other times, I feel completely disillusioned: how much courage and luck does it take to find one's niche and bravely stick to it? If even a tiny stock investment can make money, then those who go all-in, invest for years, or own their own companies as major shareholders must be making immeasurable fortunes.

And if the entire society is like this, with the wealth gap widening ever more drastically, will people still live under oppression? Won't those who are oppressed eventually become disillusioned?

What is an unfree, oppressed life? It is when you dislike what you do, yet keep spending your time and energy for a promise that seems like a blank check. Like a donkey walking in circles turning a millstone, constantly chasing a carrot that seems just out of reach but always remains at a distance. — Is that not exactly my current state?

II. The Miracle Question: When Money is No Longer an Issue
If money were truly no longer an issue for me (that is, the day I have my own steady stream of income)...

Would I sit on a pile of money and be happy? I would probably feel more confident and grounded because of a sense of personal achievement.

How would my food consumption change? I probably wouldn't buy discounted items anymore; instead, I would pursue higher standards. The same three meals a day would only be judged by the healthiness, freshness, and the care put into the food by the chef. Even if I might still feel a bit timid entering high-end restaurants, I would feel more at ease internally, not questioning my self-worth based on the waitstaff's attitude. Food would become more of a connection with nature and a unique cultural experience—like the jujube paste yam cake, sour bamboo shoot chicken skin soup, and tofu skin buns in *Dream of the Red Chamber*.

What would change about my lifestyle habits? Overall, I would want to have more experiences. I would have my own familiar small communities in different regions—like a workation or extended stays, but not aimless drifting. I would have a main thread in my life, but most of the time, I could wander without a destination. Having fresh experiences alongside a familiar rhythm, without letting peak or off-peak seasons dictate my flight purchases. I could better satisfy the idealistic, "let's go right now" child inside me—when I want to do something, as long as there is an opportunity, I must act, and no longer make excuses not to live the life I want due to a lack of money or time.

How would I arrange my time? In all likelihood, I would still want to exercise every morning—hopefully running, yoga, swimming, and table tennis if possible. But I might spend a significant amount of money to hire coaches I trust for occasional guidance, because I prefer the rhythm of "consulting periodically, but mostly practicing and accumulating on my own." I might also develop brand-new hobbies, especially less active ones like painting, photography, and music.

Would I still teach yoga? I would probably still want to teach as a way to grow and improve. But I would only open classes when the time is right and the connection is there; I wouldn't want to be an instructor just to make a living, nor would I want to aggressively promote the benefits of yoga. Video production, promotion, and organizing could be outsourced to people I trust, leaving me to focus only on the parts I'm genuinely interested in.

Would I still pursue a PhD? Possibly. But not with the goal of graduating; I would study solely what interests me, at a place where I can genuinely learn, rather than for a job search or residency status. The subjects would be limitless, not confined to established disciplines—botany, astrological metaphysics, religion, anthropology, psycho-mathematics, bacteriology, and virology could all be explored. I would hope to discuss things with people who are deeply passionate about their fields, rather than learning from those whose primary interests are professional titles and salaries.

What would my relationship with work look like? My work would serve me, being a source of value and satisfaction. I could leave at any moment and directly, boldly express my true thoughts. For repetitive or unnecessary tasks, I might hire someone I trust to handle them. I might have a team I trust; I would know the general direction of everyone's work, and they would help me execute the details. Meanwhile, I would only do what I am genuinely interested in—even if those details still consume my time and energy, it would feel like an artistic creation and a process of spiritual cultivation.

What changes would happen to my social circle? I would still hope for sincere connections, while also having a wider variety of people to interact with. Although the social circles and lifestyles of the wealthy might also be quite limited (e.g., only flying private and never waiting at airport gates). But right now, feeling internally depleted, I find it hard to fully open up to friends. I focus more on personal growth, with utility-driven, selfish time allocations, always terrified that stopping for even a moment might ruin my potential career. Socializing is performative, and relaxation is forced. The biggest luxury I could buy with financial freedom is to be genuinely relaxed, not just pretending. I might actually spend more time hanging out and chatting with friends. Not to show off my life, but because I finally feel that I've let myself off the hook, and I hope my friends can let themselves off the hook, too.

What kind of books would I read? Or would I stop reading altogether once I'm rich, and just fly directly to the author for a chat? Just kidding, this probably wouldn't change much, since even with money, I can't meet Cao Xueqin or Gabriel García Márquez. So I would still read silently, read excitedly, read secretly—reading everything I'm interested in, and closing any book I don't feel like reading at any moment. But I probably wouldn't check in on WeChat Read daily to earn free book tokens. After all, if I'm that rich, I'll just buy the annual membership directly.

III. Limitations and Epiphanies
What limitations would I still face? Even with money, you still cannot buy sincerity and love; sincerity and love still need to be guarded with courage and honesty. My health would likely improve due to material abundance and mental ease, but I still cannot escape life's unpredictabilities. My time would still flow away. I am aging, becoming slower, and constantly saying goodbye to people around me; there will still be many regrets and things I won't have time to finish.

So, to me, money means safety and freedom. What I want is perhaps not money itself, but the internal space and composure it brings, the self-confidence and certainty, and the freedom to design my own life without explaining it to anyone, without guilt, and without hesitating or fearing being different.

For me, money is to a large extent a means to buy time and freedom. However, I do not want to exhaust my time and surrender my freedom in the very process of chasing money. In other words, if I am internally grounded, no longer anxious about not working hard enough, and preserve my agency over my time and freedom, have I not already achieved or enjoyed the lifestyle of the wealthy? Or, even if I reach my financial goals and become rich, what kind of wealthy person would I be? One who is held hostage by money—the richer, the more fearful—or a carefree, wealthy idler like Jia Baoyu?

Will I actually remember what kind of life I originally wanted when that day comes? If my physical health and energy have faded by the time I have money, what experiences can money even bring me?

IV. The Ultimate Choice: The Texture of Life and "Die with Zero"
Therefore, another modern financial concept, "Die with Zero", appeals to me deeply—the idea of spending all your money before you die, letting every single cent fulfill its purpose, whether through donation or consumption.

$$ \text{Value of Life} = \sum (\text{Pure Experiences and Spiritual Energy Acquired Within Finite Time}) $$

The only truly scarce resources are time and energy. For everyone, they are finite, constantly depleting, and irreversible. It is only because time cannot be exchanged or circulated; otherwise, time would probably be the hardest currency. All other resources can be accumulated in greater quantities by investing more effort and time.

Thus, if we lose our own time for the sake of money (a brilliant capitalist rule that makes social resources run efficiently, but also causes immense inequality), it is a profound tragedy.

The choice we make, in every single moment, should always be: focus on whether this is something I am willing to trade my life for. Does this expenditure of time and energy elevate the quality and energy of my life?

If it does, then that is the most rational decision for you.
