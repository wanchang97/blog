    Observing the Chain Reaction of Emotions in My Parents and Myself

    March 9, 2026 | Translations:
    • Zh

    Dad likes to focus on the future, concerned with things like Elon Musk’s Starlink, dragon banknotes, AI, emerging trends, and making money. This is the only success he recognizes in his values.

    He demands this of those around him, and he demands it of himself.

    So when those around him fail to meet these expectations, or when he himself fails, his frustration turns into violent anger.

    He wants to control things to be as good as his expectations, but when he can’t control or understand something, he gets angry. Anger is a manifestation of having no other choice. Because if there were other solutions, we could calmly face awkward situations with self-deprecation or self-encouragement. As long as there are other ways to solve a problem, it shouldn’t trigger strong emotions. So, his outbursts are actually a sign of vulnerability, a signal that he needs help.

    Coincidentally, I’ve seen this in my father-in-law as well. For very minor things, such as packing leftovers, if it’s not done perfectly—a little spills out, a little lands on the floor, and so on—he’ll get angry and start blaming those around him, like my mother-in-law. What I observe in these middle-aged and older men seems to be the same trait: on the surface, it appears to be an unconscious suppression, belittling, or even intimidation by men towards women in a patriarchal society. However, it might actually be a very common human feeling: the inability to accept a true self that “cannot always be strong” and control everything. They are deeply vulnerable inside, so they want to shift blame, to cover up, to hide their uncontrolled side. A scene that comes to mind is Shi Tiesheng, in his early twenties, after suddenly becoming paralyzed in both legs, often smashing plates at home. It’s the weakness and anger of an old man who has lost his mobility, like one who falls and cannot get up with his cane. They will affect and transfer their emotions onto those around them, but deep down, perhaps what they truly blame isn’t the people beside them, but their own incompetence, and their desire to regain a sense of control over their lives.

    At these moments, such as when Dad suddenly loses his temper or starts blaming her, Mom often becomes scared and fearful, growing even more cautious. She expresses her helplessness through fear. However, for Dad, what he likely cares about is whether the problem or difficulty has been resolved, and whether he can still control his own life—not whether those around him are afraid of him. Or, in other words, no one wants to be feared by others. So, fear won’t solve any problems or alleviate or improve any relationships. My suggestion is for Mom to protect herself first, not to be afraid, and to give Dad quiet space and time. Mom should do what she needs to do, allowing Dad to digest his own emotions and figure out his true problems and unresolved issues. Mom often says she doesn’t want to anger Dad and doesn’t know what to do to avoid being blamed. My first point is that Mom should not be responsible for Dad’s anger. No one’s emotions can be made someone else’s responsibility. Because, as human beings, in a life filled with vanity and fame, we all get disoriented, doubt ourselves, and hope for more, better, and longer-lasting things. But we also helplessly face a day when we are powerless. Dad needs to reconcile with his desires. Mom needs to reconcile with the reality that she cannot counsel Dad. In the end, we all just need to admit the parts we cannot do. And to live with our own emotions.
