    Insights from My Personal Yoga Practice

    A summary of my personal yoga practice experience over several years, and my insights into breath, stretch, and stability.
    March 25, 2026 | Translations:
    • Zh

    Insights from My Personal Yoga Practice:

    By a strange coincidence, I started trying Yoga during the 2020 pandemic. I bought a Keep membership and followed their hot yoga sessions. After a few years of persistence, I could gradually perform many poses. The guidance from the video instructors, in my opinion, might even surpass many in-person training classes. This is based on a simple logic: any yoga instructor would want to put their best side, most detailed explanations, and most refined content into their videos. On the other hand, anyone at work will inevitably slack off. A single in-person class usually offers a relatively low real return for them. Therefore, few might teach with the mindset of making you feel you’ve gotten your money’s worth; often, classes turn into listening to the instructor show off or chat. However, in video production, instructors can gain higher returns due to the leverage principle. Additionally, even if not solely for money, but for reputation and sustainable development, they will certainly remove unnecessary chatter and present their most professional selves. I hold the same belief for other training courses, such as learning German. Well-prepared, concise, and systematic online courses are not easy to create. In offline classes, you only need to observe the reactions and feedback of a few students; they might attend because of the instructor’s reputation, or more likely, because of convenience, suitable time, location, etc. But online courses, by necessity, face competition from many other high-quality courses. If one doesn’t take it seriously, they won’t gain trust and followers. If you personally don’t have unique enough insights or concise enough summaries, you might only be able to win through the video’s first-mover advantage, location scouting, filming, or the sheer number of videos.

    Of course, if you genuinely find a suitable and trustworthy instructor, in-person classes offer irreplaceable benefits that online classes cannot, such as receiving feedback, interaction, and participation. If it’s a small group class with an instructor who provides feedback and is observant, you can indeed progress faster. However, yoga practice is not something that yields results from a single session. Continuous practice without injury or fear is the only way to progress.

    In the past two years, Keep’s hot yoga series seems to have finished updating, and they no longer appear to be investing in inviting instructors to record yoga courses. Ads have become more frequent, and in addition to membership cards, they’ve launched “unlimited practice” cards. So, I stopped following Keep. I switched to individual channels of different yoga instructors on platforms like Bilibili and YouTube. I feel like I’ve gained some good insights there too.

    The problem is that there are so many videos online that it feels like I could never finish following them all in a lifetime. They aren’t very systematic, or rather, they’re more like weight loss and fitness instructors encouraging me to keep exercising. For example, Yoga Boho Beautiful, Yoga Marison, etc. I certainly tried fitness types before, like “Zhou Liu Ye” and Pamela Reif, but they seemed somewhat superficial, focusing only on form without spirit. It wasn’t until I watched videos by yogalili, a yoga instructor with a psychology background from Peking University. I felt that her sharing wasn’t just about endless updates but had substantial content. In other words, many yoga instructor creators’ videos are designed to make you continue practicing with them and watch more videos. In contrast, yogalili’s channel videos are meant to help you turn off the screen and practice better by yourself.

    Without music or guided cues, I truly didn’t know how to practice, as I hadn’t specifically learned pose names or intentionally memorized pose sequences. My self-practice often stalled. During a brief in-person class at Mount Emei, I tried a little but was interrupted by the instructor’s lengthy discourse. Instructor Li Xiaozhong’s philosophy is profound: it’s about feeling how to be comfortable rather than getting injured through basic poses. Indeed, there’s no standard answer in Yoga, and RYT200 (Registered Yoga Teacher 200) is not a uniform exam or solution. Everyone has their teaching and practice methods. The important thing is for me to find a practice method that suits me.

    What I look forward to more is detailed explanations for specific movements, followed by an undisturbed practice session where I can feel each subtle change, and then have the opportunity to provide feedback, communicate, and summarize that part.

    And so, it comes to today. It seems I might have an opportunity to teach Yoga. I’m also in a state of pressured excitement. Suddenly, I felt motivated to turn off the screen and practice on my own. The poses in my practice weren’t very complete. It might truly be Vinyasa Yoga, moving from one pose to another according to my body’s inclinations; my brain wanted to control, but didn’t quite know the “right” way. So, I started my practice somewhat haphazardly. Perhaps this time, due to pollen allergies, I won’t be able to push myself to become a yoga instructor. However, being able to start self-practice is a huge step forward. Because, imagining myself as a teacher, I’ve been constantly thinking about what I can share and what I want to share. This is a summary of both my experience following classes, watching videos, and my own reflections:

    1. The essence of Yoga or meditation is to bring your focus to the here, the now, and your body. In other words, it’s about focusing on your breath, not your greedy thoughts or fleeting desires that play like a constantly-on television. And indeed, observing your breath at every moment is not an easy task. Thus, we have the practice of yoga asanas; those seemingly difficult, unstable poses compel you to focus on your breath, to the present moment. Because if your mind is still preoccupied with other things, you won’t be able to maintain stability.

    2. What I believe is most important in Yoga are breath, stretch, and stability. In the past, when following classes, I often started exhaling when I was guided to inhale; I frequently did the opposite. I recall Li Sisi’s yoga class beginning with her saying, “I won’t tell you whether to inhale or exhale; just follow your own breathing rhythm.” This is absolutely correct. We are not here to learn how to breathe, but to observe the breath we already possess. We don’t need to force ourselves to follow external breathing cues. However, in asana practice, there are still some small tips that can help us inject breath into every movement. These are insights gained from watching yogalili’s videos. Inhale, the corresponding action or awareness is stretch, upward, and forward. Feel each inhale bringing us 1 millimeter or 1 micrometer more stretch. Exhale, the corresponding action or awareness is stability, relaxation, the feeling of rooting downward and being supported. Feel each exhale connecting us better to the ground, making us stable, safe, and reliable. If you stay in the same position for several breaths without a distinct upward or downward movement, you should still consciously, during the inhale, stretch outward from your chest and abdomen, and during the exhale, root downward from your chest and abdomen towards the ground.

    3. This leads to the question, how do you judge whether you are overexerting yourself or making an effort that improves you? What is “too much” and “going too far,” and what is facing hardship without escaping it? This is a common question in life, and also in yoga poses. Let’s first focus on Yoga. Typically, the stretch of a body part (which may correspond to flexibility) or stability (which may correspond to muscle strength, core strength) has many variations. And different people, at different times, have body conditions suitable for different levels of stretching and exercise. It’s comparable to the weight lifted in weightlifting, the height cleared in high jump, or the angle of a freestyle spin. This judgment can only be based on our own understanding and awareness of our body and mind. This is something that others, even an instructor, cannot decide for you. Just like Gu Ailing (Eileen Gu) alone can decide what her next move will be, based on her self-knowledge. Yogalili proposed a supplementary method for judgment: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8GptZRQUTwE&t=15s , which is, can you maintain smooth breathing in a certain pose? Can you infuse breath into that moment’s pose instead of holding your breath? If you can breathe smoothly but feel somewhat tired, you can stay in that static pose for a few breaths. If you cannot breathe smoothly, return to a pose where you can breathe normally.

    4. This brings us to what I just mentioned: for each part of the body’s stretch, we have many variations. This is content I really want to summarize for my own practice or teaching. I once followed a Vinyasa Yoga class on Keep, and I found it very interesting. She started from a supine position, feeling the cat arch (supine cat pose) and dog arch (supine cow pose). Speaking of which, B.K.S. Iyengar’s “Light on Pranayama” states that the most difficult yoga pose is not Wheel Pose, Shoulderstand, Headstand, or Handstand, but Savasana, the Corpse Pose. When I first heard this translation, I found it quite strange why the word “corpse” was used. In essence, it is indeed about experiencing the boundary between life and death. The difficulty of this pose is like an actor’s most challenging task being the portrayal of psychological drama. Its external form is easy to imitate; many other poses, even if we don’t fully infuse breath into them with mind-body unity, might look similar externally. But Savasana truly tests our understanding of Yoga. It’s about consciously, without falling asleep, slowly withdrawing your senses, gradually slowing your heartbeat, until you enter a state of no-self.
