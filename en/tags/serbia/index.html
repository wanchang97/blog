    Serbia Ski Trip Guide: Belgrade’s Sightseeing and Kopaonik’s Skiing

    Foreword: A Spontaneous Surprise\nThis winter, my mother and I spent two “relaxed” weeks in Serbia. From leisurely strolls in Belgrade to an impromptu week of skiing in Kopaonik, this trip, originally conceived on the spur of the moment due to visa issues, brought us unexpected joy.\nThe cost of living here is extremely low, and there isn’t the sense of unrest that some rumors suggested; instead, it is filled with a unique, righteous sense of security characteristic of socialist countries.\n\n# History and Present: Navigating Alliances After the Dust Settles\nOver the past century, this region has been plagued by continuous wars, conflicts among three major religions, and successive struggles for dominance by great empires. Upon landing, you can immediately see “Kosovo is Serbia” slogans, as if a single spark could reignite conflict. Border disputes persist after the Kosovo War of Independence in 2008.\n\nA staggering set of data: the capital, Belgrade, has experienced 115 wars and been razed to the ground 44 times in its history, a record unparalleled in world urban history. Walking through Belgrade’s Old Town, it’s hard to ignore the black marks on the exterior walls of buildings and the truncated tree branches. They don’t all speak of bombardment. Some are indeed the charred scars left by the 1999 air raids, still standing in the city center like solidified pain; but more often, they are layers of soot, exhaust fumes, and oxidation from a century of time. These varying shades of gray and black adhere to the exquisite Austro-Hungarian style carvings, making the entire city look like a faded charcoal drawing. This is completely unlike the usual rustic and elegant European towns one might see. I dare not imagine what Ukraine, where war has not yet ended, must look like now.\n\nYet, at the same time, precisely because it stands at the crossroads of East-West rivalry, it can simultaneously integrate three religious beliefs and accommodate different political views. Contemporary Serbia plays all sides among various powers. Despite diverse internal voices, its good relations with China and Russia, along with its openness to the EU and the US, have injected strong momentum into its economic growth. Not to mention that Serbia has offered visa-free access to Chinese ordinary passport holders since 2017, actively seeks to join the EU, and is incredibly friendly to the US. For example, by the end of 2025, the Serbian parliament just passed a proposal by 130 votes to 40 to build a Trump Hotel on the site of the former Yugoslav Ministry of Defense building, which was bombed by NATO. The project also received personal support from Serbian President Vučić, who classified the reconstruction as an emergency project and lifted the cultural heritage protection status of the site. This incident sparked strong local discontent and protests. Whether it shows a lack of principles, a failure to protect and respect history, or flexible action prioritizing economic development is open to interpretation. One can feel that the museums, history, and culture here indeed lack the steadfastness and inheritance found in Europe; government renovation of museums often only begins after multiple rounds of protests. But how can I criticize a nation constantly ravaged by war, constantly migrating and seeking refuge, for not knowing how to protect and uphold its culture? At the same time, there are also many voices now that believe Europe is generally too rigid and inflexible, leading to slower development.\n\nHere, we will not discuss current affairs for now. The main purpose is to help everyone “go on a trip at a moment’s notice” by organizing practical travel tips. The following Chenhe’s Blog - Comprehensive Skiing Guide to Serbia was very helpful in my trip planning. Inspired by it, I want to add my own Serbia travel and skiing experiences for 2026, hoping to provide some reference for friends who wish to visit Serbia.\n\n\n# Serbia Travel Basic Guide: Pre-trip Preparations\n\n## 🛂 Entry and Visa\nVisa-free for Chinese passports, the process is extremely smooth. Essentially no queuing, no difficulty, just stamp and enter. Currently, Serbia is a non-Schengen country in the Balkan region of Europe, so flying directly from China does not require a Schengen visa.\n\n## 📶 Network and Data SIM Card\n### International Roaming from Domestic Carriers - Not Recommended\n1. Signal Issues: Roaming signal is unstable and often degrades.\n2. “Firewall” Still Exists: Using domestic roaming, you cannot directly access Google Maps, ChatGPT, or Gemini, which is critical for independent travel (checking maps/instant translation).\nSo, it’s better to connect to the airport WiFi temporarily and buy a SIM card at the airport.\n\n### International Travel eSIM plan - Convenient and Flexible, Slightly Higher Price\nSuitable for travel to many countries. Currently, most domestic mobile phones generally use physical dual SIM cards and do not support eSIM, while American versions are all virtual cards and cannot use physical cards. European versions of phones have one physical and one virtual card. Please check your phone type to confirm if it supports eSIM.\n\n### Purchasing a Local Physical SIM Card or eSIM Card\n* Purchase Channels: Upon landing in Serbia, there are Yettel and A1 operator counters in the airport baggage claim area. Prices are transparent, no overcharging, and real-name activation with your passport is done on site.\n* Package Reference (February 2026):\n * Purely within Serbia:\n For example, Yettel offers a tourist-friendly package of 100GB data for 700 RSD for 15 days; 50GB data for 500 RSD for 7 days, etc.\n \n \n * Balkan Roaming: If you plan to visit Montenegro or Bosnia and Herzegovina, you can choose 50GB (within Serbia) + 8GB (roaming) for 1400 RSD (30 days). Note: Such packages sometimes require visiting an urban branch office to activate.\n* Call Needs: The above packages are mostly data-only. If you need to contact a landlord, it’s recommended to download the operator’s App to top up for per-minute billing; or use WhatsApp, which consumes data, for communication.\n\n## 🚌 Transportation\n\n* Airport Bus (A1 Shuttle): Requires extra payment (about 400 RSD/person), can go directly to the city center. The vehicles are older but very fast.\n* City Bus: Starting January 1, 2025, all Serbian public transport is free (including for tourists). Buses are frequent; although there are many passengers, it’s very convenient.\n* Long-distance Bus: Google Maps often cannot display transportation options between different towns, often showing only driving routes and walking routes. Intercity buses in Serbia are operated by dozens of private companies (such as Lasta, Jugoprevoz, Fudeks, etc.). Most of these companies do not provide real-time or static timetables in the format required by Google.\nAlternative solutions:\n1. BAS Official Booking Website\n2. Local Bus Website, you can now book tickets directly on the local website. However, for buses operated by companies like Lasta, booked online, you will need to pay an additional platform ticket fee of about 500 RSD per person at the BAS station. If you buy tickets directly at the bus station, you don’t need to pay the extra platform fee, but the total price might be similar.\n3. Kopaonik Shuttle Bus\nIn addition to regular buses, there are many private minivans/shared transfers (Shared Transfer) for routes to and from Kopaonik. For example, Terra Travel or Gea Tours offer “door-to-door” service (picking you up from your Belgrade accommodation and taking you to the ski resort). These services cannot be found on any map software and need to be booked on their official websites or via phone/WhatsApp.\n* Taxi: It is recommended to use Yandex Go or Pink Taxi. Prices are transparent, which can effectively prevent being overcharged when hailing a taxi on the street.\n\n## 💰 Currency Exchange and Payment\nThe Serbian currency is the Dinar (RSD), and its exchange rate with the Euro is relatively stable. Euros are also generally accepted. However, many people prefer cash for payments, especially in guesthouses, so it’s necessary to bring enough cash.\n\n* Strategy: Carry Euro cash + multi-currency credit card.\n* Currency Exchange: There are automatic exchange machines in the airport baggage claim area, with transparent exchange rates.\n* Scenarios requiring cash: Airport buses, long-distance buses, taxis, some Booking.com guesthouse fees, ski equipment rental, instructor fees, and small restaurants.\n* Budget Reference: For two people traveling for two weeks, exchanging about 500 Euros worth of RSD cash is usually sufficient.\n\n## 🧳 Packing: Onion-style layering and German Type F (Schuko) plugs\n* Climate: Winters are mostly above freezing, but it’s extremely windy! Where the Sava and Danube rivers converge, the swirling winds are wild, making it more exhilarating than the seaside.\n* Attire: “Onion-style” layering is recommended; bring everything from short-sleeved shirts to shell jackets.\n* Must-read for Skiers: Snowboard beginners are advised to bring their own professional hip and knee protectors (don’t bring cheap online “turtle” pads, they easily shift and provide insufficient protection). In Belgrade, there are a few Decathlon stores, but choices for professional ski shops are limited; good gear usually relies on online shopping.\n\n\n# Belgrade’s Sightseeing and Dining: Markets, Ball Games, Yugoslav Drama Theatre\n\nGeneral historical and cultural introductions can be obtained from AI; here, I will only discuss places that I found enjoyable. We wanted to experience more of local life, so we did not choose luxurious places like hotels or spas, but rather guesthouses, local swimming pools, local bakeries, and markets. We did not take taxis, opting instead for public transport and walking.\n## 🏠 Accommodation Layout and Recommendations\nI booked two different guesthouses in Belgrade through Booking.com, both offering great value, so you can choose according to your preferences.\n\nBelgrade city center is at the confluence of the Sava and Danube rivers (Ušće, meaning “confluence”). Belgrade can be divided into east and west sides by the Sava River.\n\nBelgrade (Belgrade) Area Comparison\n| Area | Characteristics | Suitable for |\n| :— | :— | :— |\n| Old Town (East Side) | Crowded but lively, similar to Barcelona. Close to attractions, markets, shopping centers. | Tourists who enjoy bustling local life and walking to attractions. |\n| New Belgrade (West Side) | Spacious, modern. Features Ušće Park, stadiums, many new buildings under construction. | Those who prefer quietness, open spaces, or have business needs with large companies. |\n\n\n## Sports and Sporting Events\n#### Belgrade Arena\nEuroLeague Basketball games are held periodically.\nWater polo matches are also held periodically. Water polo is Serbia’s national sport.\nOfficial booking website is: Belgrade 2026\nIf tickets are sold out, you can check secondary market websites like viagogo.\n> 💡 Tips: Ticket prices on viagogo often drop very low about seven hours before a game, so you might not need to rush to buy tickets on the secondary market in advance; you can try to find last-minute deals.\n\n\n#### Tašmajdan Pool\nWe visited a swimming pool here, at local prices, 600 RSD/person for three hours. It closes at noon, opening in separate morning and afternoon sessions. If you want fitness, swimming, and spa, it costs 2200 RSD/person.\n\nIt was interesting that although the swimming pool is Olympic-sized (50 meters long), people here don’t swim the full length; instead, they swim across the short width. Everyone takes a short lane and swims back and forth.\n\n## Culture and Performances\n### JDP (Yugoslav Drama Theatre)\n\nYou can check the program schedule at https://www.jdp.rs/. Often, tickets shown as sold out online might still be available if you ask at the physical box office. The performances are often innovative, authentic, and unconventional. The language is usually Serbian, with some productions including English subtitles in the middle. While you shouldn’t expect to understand everything, the body language, movements, music, and stage are all very immersive.\n\n### Museums\nThe Museum of Contemporary Art is the sawtooth-shaped building located in Ušće Park. Admission is free on the first Wednesday of every month and annually on May 18th.\nThe National Museum of Serbia offers free admission every Sunday.\n\n## Food and Specialty Recommendations\n\n#### Belgrade’s Markets (Pijaca), Fish Market, and Corner Bakeries\n\n##### Belgrade’s Markets\nKalenić Green Market (Kalenić Pijaca)\nYou can try local homemade Kajmak (clotted cream) and Ajvar (red pepper spread).\n\n##### Belgrade’s Fish Market\nZemun Farmers Market (Zemunska Pijaca)\n\n\n##### Bakery (Pekara)\nThe freshest bread is found at random corner bakeries. Just find any local Pekara on the street, buy a sesame-sprinkled Đevrek (bread ring), which is very similar to Turkish Simit, and pair it with a bottle of pink cow’s milk yogurt – that’s the soul of a local breakfast.\n\n\n#### Belgrade Local Cuisine\n\nOne Michelin-recommended restaurant, Iva New Balkan Cuisine, specializes in innovative preparations of Serbian local dishes featuring meat and dairy. Prices are moderate, and reservations are required at least one day in advance. Lunch service starts early.\n\nThe one-Michelin-star restaurant Langouste requires advance reservation. Failure to show up after booking will incur a 3000 RSD charge.\n\n#### Zemun District River Fish Restaurants\nOne is the century-old restaurant Šaran, which is a bit pricey but offers excellent flavor, with fish soup and whole fish. Fresh river fish caught from the nearby Danube River are highly recommended.\n\n\nAnother is a casual fish shop, Fish Butik. They cook only after you order, and all orders are for takeout, but the prices are very good. Local residents often order here, and the food is ready for pick-up in about half an hour. Its menu is entirely in Serbian; we mostly ordered based on photos and translation.\nHere, you can choose from four types of river fish:\n* carp\n* catfish\n* trout\n* perch\nAfter choosing, you can select the cooking method: grilled or fried.\nYou can also choose side dishes or potatoes.\nThis shop only accepts cash. If you eat there directly, the owner will borrow two forks from next door.\n\n\n#### Belgrade Chinese Food\nThe few Chinese restaurants we casually entered were unexpectedly delicious and authentic. (Though we’re not very picky eaters, perhaps Chinese food is simply satisfying.)\nThey offer a wide range, including dumplings, roujiamo, liangpi, hand-pulled noodles, hot pot, and malatang.\n\n\n\n# Kopaonik Skiing: Beginner’s Guide\n\nMy skiing advice is more suited for beginners, as we didn’t explore the entire mountain all week, focusing mainly on the beginner’s magic carpet. We went up the mountain twice and did one night ski with an instructor. My mother and I are both quite timid, and it was our first time learning to ski; my mother on twin-tips, and I on a snowboard. We encountered several challenges, but I’m writing this down for reference.\n\n## Insurance\nOf course, when purchasing insurance, one hopes not to make a claim, but it also allows for embracing new experiences with peace of mind. I recommend arranging two types of insurance: Travel Insurance and Ski Insurance.\n\n### Travel Insurance\nTravel insurance varies greatly, and my mother’s and my situations were different. Especially since my mother had only purchased Schengen Area insurance before coming to Germany to visit me, it was impossible to buy travel insurance for someone residing in Europe traveling abroad, nor for someone residing in mainland China traveling abroad (because travel insurance generally must be purchased before departure, and my mother was already in Germany).\ Finally, with the help of Gemini, we found a few companies that offer travel insurance for people who have already started their trip, regardless of their place of residence. We purchased from Heymondo and selected “already abroad.” Their requirement is that the policy takes effect 72 hours after purchase. Since we haven’t used it, I don’t know the specifics of their claims process. It was just a viable option we found for adding insurance while already traveling. In the future, it’s best to buy global travel insurance in China; for a similar price, it can save a lot of trouble.\n\nFor those residing long-term in Europe, travel insurance prices often vary depending on whether the destination includes the US and Canada. Also, a one-off travel insurance policy might not be cheaper than an annual one. It can also be more cost-effective for couples or families to buy together. You can compare options on check24. This time, I purchased an annual worldwide policy from Europe Assistance.\n\n\n### Ski Insurance\n\nGenerally, purchasing a ski pass for the ski lift will include daily insurance for that ski resort. At different ski resorts, there might be additional separate charges. However, this local coverage is indeed more direct and reliable.\n\nFrequent skiers who reside in Europe can purchase Ski Insurance DSV (Deutsche Ski Verein). It offers packages that cover worldwide ski travel risks, damage or loss of ski equipment, and third-party liability for injuring others. This might be the most cost-effective insurance type I’ve found.\n\nSki equipment rental sometimes includes additional insurance fees. If you already have DSV insurance, you don’t need to purchase additional rental shop insurance. Otherwise, it’s advisable to purchase it all at once. The Kopaonik ski center is a bit more casual; the rental shop I used didn’t mention equipment insurance. Sometimes they take a photo of your ID, sometimes they just ask for your ski instructor’s name, and usually, there’s no deposit.\n\n\n## Accommodation, Transportation, and Ski Rental:\n\nConsider accommodation, transportation, and ski rental together with your skiing level.\nIf you are looking for value and staying in a nearby town requiring driving or bus commutes, you can rent equipment at the ski center and store it there daily.\n\n### Accommodation\nIf you want a ski-in/ski-out experience, especially as a beginner, the main beginner magic carpets are located at Kopaonik Ski Center. So, it’s best to book a hotel near Ski Center Kopaonik. A well-known four-star hotel here is Gorski. You can also find nearby guesthouses through Booking.com for more reasonable prices, often with washing machines and kitchens. This ski center has affordable chain supermarkets like Maxi and Idea, making life convenient even without breakfast.\nInfoKop\nThis site compiles all useful information about skiing in Kopaonik: weather, activities, ski pass prices, and last-minute guesthouse prices. However, last-minute information is only in Serbian. The English content provides sufficient, timely updated information on lift operations, slope conditions, and other details skiers need.\n\n### Transportation\nTransportation at the ski resort includes taxis, ski resort buses, self-driving, and cable cars. Taxis are a bit expensive and might overcharge; I didn’t dare try. Ski resort buses run hourly, and tickets are bought with cash on board. Cable cars run continuously during daytime operating hours.\n\nIf you are an experienced skier, at least comfortable with T-bars and platter lifts, there are many more ski-in/ski-out guesthouse options in nearby villages, detailed in Chenhe’s Blog. Even if they are located in towns different from the ski center, they can still be nearly ski-in/ski-out. Obviously, beginners should not put this pressure on themselves.\n\nBecause the nearby ski bus runs hourly and charges extra, if you truly need to commute daily by ski bus, you can rent equipment at the Ski Center and store it for free at the rental place. To save energy and enhance the skiing experience, prioritize ski-in/ski-out accommodation.\n\nThe village of Brzeće offers accommodation half the price of Ski Center. Although it’s a nearby village, a new Gondola lift has been built, with more frequent services than the bus, allowing for ascent and descent. However, note that the last Gondola usually runs at 16:00, and after this time, you’ll still need to take a ski bus back.\n\n### Ski Rental\nHere, I recommend a lodging-plus-restaurant that I saw during my skiing trip, Mašinac Resort. It’s located right next to green run number 22 and resembles a youth hostel. Due to its low prices and good location, it’s often fully booked. You can also rent equipment, hire instructors, and store your gear there. The prices are arguably the lowest in the entire resort: ski rental is 800 RSD/day, snowboard is 1000 RSD/day, helmet 200 RSD/day, and goggles 200 RSD/day.\n\n\nMany rental shops down the mountain have higher prices, though renting for several days can get you a discount. Below is a quote I received from a rental shop down the mountain.\n\n\nOf course, with lower prices comes lower equipment quality. If you don’t mind and just want to experience it, you can contact them directly. Their English isn’t great, but they are very enthusiastic. Their instructor fees are also relatively low, around 30 Euros/hour. However, they are not officially certified instructors, even though they constantly take groups of young Serbians staying at the hostel up the mountain to learn skiing. People in Eastern Europe often have a collective personality where rules don’t seem to be too restrictive; however, this also gives rise to a vibrancy and freedom beyond rigidity.\n\nIf you want a rental shop with better quality equipment, my instructor recommended a place with moderate prices, which was indeed more professional, especially with a wider selection of snowboards.\n\n> 💡 Tips: Although run 22 is a green run, it still requires taking a platter lift up and down. Beginners might find it difficult to confidently get on and off the lift, so my mother and I still had to carry our rented gear to the mid-mountain to return it.\n\n## Ski Pass and Instructors\nThe types of ski pass combinations can be found on the InfoKop website, where purchasing online offers at least a 5% discount compared to buying on-site.\nHigh-resolution Ski Map\nSki Pass Price List 2025-2026\nSki pass combinations are diverse: there are beginner-only passes for single use, half-day passes, full-day passes, night skiing passes, multi-day passes, and more. For night skiing, only lift 7 is open. This is a blue run with excellent snow quality. There is artificial snowmaking and grooming every day, and music at night, creating a great atmosphere.\n\nThe beginner slopes here are all near the ski center, served by magic carpets. There are very gentle “baby slopes” and beginner slopes with some gradient, as shown by the green indicated areas in the picture. The area cordoned off by BMW billboards is the baby slope for twin-tip skiers. The green-marked area with a gradient is specifically for snowboard beginners to practice. If you hire a ski instructor, you will usually meet them at the beginner slopes. A normal ski instructor costs around 45 Euros/hour. If you also rent equipment at the same shop, there are usually more discounts.\n\n\nIt’s advisable to buy beginner passes for the first few days, then upgrade to half-day or multi-day passes as your skills improve! Beginners who are unsure can consult their ski instructor to decide whether to go up the mountain or stay on the beginner slopes for the day.\n\n\n## Relaxation Activities: Spa & Wellness\nWhen learning to ski gets too intense, it’s essential to know how to relax.\nWe tried the four-star Gorski Hotel, which advertises even in Belgrade. For non-hotel guests, it costs 3900 RSD/person for a morning. You pay directly on floor B3. The pool and hot springs were decent, the saunas were complete, and there was also a novel relaxation room with a waterbed-like feature.\n\nGorski Hotel’s B1 floor has an Italian Fine Dining restaurant with good portions and creative presentations. There’s a raspberry rose dessert that comes with dry ice, making for great photo opportunities.
