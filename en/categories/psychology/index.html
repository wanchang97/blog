    Observing the Emotional Chain Reactions of My Parents and Myself

    Dad likes to focus on the future, caring about things like Elon Musk, Starlink, Dragon Banknotes, AI, emerging trends, and making money. This is the only success he recognizes in his values. He demands this of those around him, and also of himself. So when those around him can’t achieve it, or when he himself can’t, his frustration turns into rage. He wants to control things to be as good as he expects, but when he can’t control or understand something, he gets angry. Anger is what people resort to when they have no other choice. Because if there were other solutions, we could face awkward situations calmly with self-deprecating humor or self-encouragement. As long as there are other ways to solve a problem, it shouldn’t trigger significant emotions. So, his outbursts are actually a manifestation of vulnerability, a signal that he needs help. Coincidentally, I’ve observed this in my father-in-law as well. Over very minor things, like packing leftovers, if it’s not done perfectly—maybe a bit spills outside or on the floor—he gets angry and starts blaming those around him, like my mother-in-law. What I observe in these middle-aged and elderly men seems to be the same trait. Superficially, it appears to be an unconscious suppression, belittling, or even intimidation of women by men in a patriarchal society. However, it might actually be a very common human sentiment, namely, the inability to accept a self that isn’t “strong and in control all the time”. They are deeply vulnerable, so they want to shift blame, cover up, and hide their out-of-control side. A scene that comes to mind is Shi Tiesheng, in his early twenties, after suddenly becoming paralyzed in both legs, often smashing plates at home. It’s akin to the weakness and anger of an old man, a “person” who has lost mobility, falling and being unable to stand up with a cane. They will let their emotions affect, and even transfer them to, those around them, but deep down, perhaps they are not truly blaming those around them, but rather wanting to overcome their own incompetence and regain a sense of control over their lives. And Mom, at such moments—for example, when Dad suddenly loses his temper, or when Dad starts blaming her—becomes scared and fearful; she becomes even more cautious. She expresses her helplessness through fear. However, for Dad, what he cares about is whether the problem or difficulty has been resolved; he cares about whether he can still control his own life. It’s not about whether those around him are afraid of him. Or, in other words, no one wants to be feared by others. Therefore, fear won’t solve any problems, nor will it alleviate or improve any relationship. My suggestion is for Mom to protect herself first, and not to be afraid. She can give Dad quiet space and time first. Mom should do what she needs to do, letting Dad process his own emotions and figure out his true problems and emotional knots. Mom often says she doesn’t want to anger Dad, and she doesn’t know what she does that often leads to criticism. My first point is that Mom should not be responsible for Dad’s anger. No one’s emotions can be made someone else’s responsibility. Because as human beings, in a life filled with vanity and fame, we all get dizzy, doubt ourselves, and hope for more, better, and longer-lasting things. But we also helplessly face a day when we are powerless. Dad needs to reconcile with his desires. Mom needs to reconcile with the reality that she cannot counsel Dad. Ultimately, we all need to admit that there are things we cannot do and learn to live with our emotions.
